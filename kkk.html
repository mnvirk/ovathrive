What is PCOS?

Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) is a hormonal disorder affecting women of reproductive age. It is marked by irregular periods, excess male hormones (androgens), and polycystic ovaries. Common symptoms include weight gain, acne, hair thinning, and fertility issues.

PCOS is linked to insulin resistance and can lead to long-term risks like diabetes, heart disease, and endometrial cancer. Early diagnosis and lifestyle changes—like a healthy diet, exercise, and medical care—can help manage PCOS effectively.

Symptoms of PCOS

  • Irregular or absent periods
  • Excessive hair growth (Hirsutism)
  • Severe acne and oily skin
  • Weight gain and difficulty losing weight
  • Hair thinning (Androgenic alopecia)
  • Dark patches on skin (Acanthosis Nigricans)
  • Mood disorders: anxiety, depression
  • Sleep problems and sleep apnea
  • Infertility and pregnancy complications

Causes & Risk Factors

  • Insulin resistance
  • Hormonal imbalance
  • Genetics
  • Chronic low-grade inflammation
  • Obesity

Diagnosis & Tests

PCOS is diagnosed using the Rotterdam Criteria (2003). A woman must have two out of the following three:

  • Irregular or absent periods
  • Signs of hyperandrogenism (clinical or biochemical)
  • Polycystic ovaries seen in ultrasound

Other tests include blood tests (hormones, insulin, lipids), pelvic ultrasound, and thyroid or prolactin checks.

Treatment Options

  • Lifestyle Changes: Weight loss, low-glycemic diet, regular exercise
  • Medications: Birth control pills, Metformin, anti-androgens, fertility drugs
  • Other Therapies: Laser hair removal, ovarian drilling

Managing PCOS involves long-term commitment to a healthy lifestyle and regular medical care.
